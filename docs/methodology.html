Methodology

Environmental Health Adaptation Gap Index

A county-level screening index for identifying where environmental-health risks overlap with weaker adaptive capacity.

Back to dashboard

1. Research objective

This dashboard identifies U.S. counties where climate/natural hazard burden, air pollution exposure, health vulnerability, and weaker adaptive capacity overlap. The goal is to highlight counties that may deserve closer attention for climate adaptation, environmental-health preparedness, and public-health investment.

2. Unit of analysis

The unit of analysis is the county. County-level analysis allows FEMA risk, EPA air quality, CDC PLACES health outcomes, and ACS adaptive-capacity variables to be harmonized using county FIPS codes.

3. Domains

Climate/natural hazard burden uses FEMA National Risk Index indicators such as risk score, expected annual loss score, social vulnerability score, and hazard-specific fields when available. Air pollution exposure uses EPA AirData indicators such as AQI, PM2.5, ozone, and unhealthy air days. Health vulnerability uses CDC PLACES measures such as asthma, COPD, coronary heart disease, poor physical health, poor mental health, and depression. Adaptive capacity deficit combines vulnerability variables such as poverty, uninsured rate, older population, limited vehicle access, limited internet access, renter share, and the inverse of community resilience where available.

4. Index construction

Each variable is converted to a national percentile rank. Higher percentiles indicate greater burden, vulnerability, or adaptive-capacity deficit. Domain scores are calculated as averages of percentile-ranked variables. The final Environmental Health Adaptation Gap Index is calculated as:

EHAGI = 0.30(Climate/Natural Hazard Burden)
      + 0.25(Air Pollution Exposure)
      + 0.25(Health Vulnerability)
      + 0.20(Adaptive Capacity Deficit)

5. Interpretation

A higher EHAGI score means a county has a stronger overlap of environmental-health risk and weaker adaptive capacity. The index is not a causal estimate and should not be interpreted as evidence that environmental exposures caused health outcomes.

6. Suggested empirical extension

A PhD paper can use the dashboard as data infrastructure. A first paper can describe where adaptation gaps are concentrated and how they differ by state, region, and rural/urban status. A later causal paper could evaluate whether adaptation funding, air pollution regulation, disaster-preparedness grants, or infrastructure investments reduce measured adaptation gaps over time.
